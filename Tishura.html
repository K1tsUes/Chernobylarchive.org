Historical biography

Vladimir Tishura

As a Senior Firefighter of the 6th Paramilitary Fire Department of Pripyat (SVPCH-6), his role on the night of April 26, 1986, and his heroic actions following the explosions—including climbing directly to the highly radioactive roof of the ventilation block to extinguish fires threatening Unit 3 made him one of the most critical and tragic figures of the nuclear disaster.

Birth: 15th of December 1959
Death: 10th of May 1986

First Years and Formation

Vladimir Ivanovich Tishura was born on December 15, 1959, in Siversky, located within the Leningrad Oblast of the Russian SSR. In 1974, he graduated from the local Naval Shipbuilding School in his hometown, and by 1977, he completed his technical studies at the Khabarovsk Professional Polytechnic School (MFTU-30). Following his education, he served as a squad leader in the Soviet Internal Troops where he attained the military rank of Sergeant, refining his operational discipline and physical readiness for emergency response scenarios

Chernobyl Carreer

Following his military service, Tishura dedicated his skills to civil defense and firefighting. He moved to Ukraine and secured a position as a firefighter within the 6th Paramilitary Fire Department (SVPCH-6) based in Pripyat. In this deployment, he worked closely with regional emergency units, specializing in heavy infrastructure fire prevention and direct tactical responses near the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant.

Day of the disaster

In the early hours of April 26, 1986, Tishura was among the first responders dispatched under the immediate command of Lieutenant Viktor Kibenok, arriving at the scene at 01:35 AM. He was tasked with climbing to the highly radioactive roof of the ventilation block to extinguish fires threatening Unit 3. Due to the intense and invisible radiation field emanating from the exposed core, Tishura absorbed an extreme dose, becoming the first fireman to collapse on the roof from severe vomiting and muscle failure.

Mitigation actions and Death

Despite the rapid onset of radiation poisoning, Tishura's early efforts successfully prevented the fire from spreading to the neighboring reactor. He was assisted off the roof by his comrade Vasily Ignatenko and rushed to Pripyat Sanitary Unit No. 126 before being evacuated. Diagnosed with 4th-degree Acute Radiation Syndrome alongside extensive intestinal injuries, Tishura passed away on May 10, 1986, at Hospital Number 6 in Moscow. He was posthumously honored with the Soviet Order of the Red Banner and later awarded the Title of Hero of Ukraine.
