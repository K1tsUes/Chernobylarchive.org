Chronology of the Plant (1970 - 2000)

1970

Foundation: Following the official decree by the Soviet Ministry of Energy, site selection was finalized near the Pripyat River due to its abundant water supply. In February 1970, construction officially commenced on both the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant and the neighboring "Atomograd" (atomic city) of Pripyat, designed to house thousands of future workers and engineers. The project required massive earthworks and engineering prep, culminating in August 1972 when the first cubic meter of concrete was poured into the foundation of Reactor Unit 1. Over the next few years, the landscape was drastically transformed with the construction of a massive 22-square-kilometer artificial cooling pond, laying down the technical foundation for what was planned to become one of the largest nuclear energy hubs in the Soviet Union.

1977 - 1983

Commissioning & Expansion: Following the launch of Reactor 1, the site entered a phase of intense, round-the-clock industrial development to meet the Soviet Union's aggressive energy quotas. In December 1978, Reactor Unit 2 was successfully connected to the grid, making the complex highly productive despite declassified archives later revealing that the KGB noted multiple structural deviations and rushed assembly during this phase. Construction then shifted to the second generation of the plant—Units 3 and 4—which shared a single, massive building structure. Reactor 3 went online in December 1981, followed by the completion of Reactor 4, which was synchronized with the power grid on December 20, 1983. By the end of this period, the Chernobyl Power Complex was generating 4,000 megawatts of electricity, supplying roughly 10% of Ukraine's power, while thousands of workers were already breaking ground on the foundations for Reactors 5 and 6.

April 26, 1986

The Catastrophe – Anatomy of a Meltdown: The disaster at Reactor 4 was not a sudden explosion, but the culmination of a delayed safety test, severe RBMK design flaws, and fatal operational decisions. The test was designed to determine whether the reactor's steam turbines could generate enough residual electricity to power the emergency cooling pumps during a blackout before the diesel generators kicked in. Ironically, it was a test meant to improve safety.

The Fatal Delay: Originally scheduled for the daytime shift on April 25, the test was suddenly postponed by the regional grid controller in Kiev to meet end-of-month electricity demands. This forced the test into the night shift of April 25–26—a crew that had not been fully briefed on the complex procedure. At 11:10 PM, the power reduction resumed, but due to operator error and a build-up of xenon-135 (a neutron-absorbing byproduct nicknamed "reactor poison"), the reactor's power plummeted to a dangerously unstable low level of 30 MW thermal, near total stall.

The Xenon Trap and Unstable Configuration: Anxious to complete the test, the operators manually withdrew almost all of the reactor's boron carbide control rods from the core to force the power back up. They managed to stabilize it at 200 MW, which was still far below the 700 MW minimum safety threshold required by the test protocols. At this point, the reactor was structurally blind and hydraulically unstable, with water flow fluctuating wildly. Despite multiple automatic alarms warning of critical instability, the safety systems were manually bypassed to prevent the reactor from shutting itself down before the test could even begin.

01:23:04 – The Test Begins: The turbine valves were closed, cutting off steam to the generators. As the cooling pumps slowed down, less water entered the core. In an RBMK reactor, less water leads to increased steam bubbles (voids). Because water acts as a neutron absorber in this design, fewer voids meant *more* nuclear reactions—a dangerous phenomenon known as a positive void coefficient. This triggered a rapid, localized surge in power inside the core.

01:23:40 – The Fatal Scram (AZ-5): Realizing the reactor's power was spiking exponentially, the shift supervisor ordered an emergency shutdown by pressing the AZ-5 button. This command was designed to fully insert all 211 control rods back into the core to kill the reaction. However, the RBMK control rods had a fundamental design flaw: their tips were made of graphite. As the rods entered the core, the graphite tips displaced the remaining water first, briefly *accelerating* the nuclear reaction instead of stopping it. This "positive scram" effect caused an uncontrollable power surge that spiked to over 33,000 MW thermal—more than ten times the reactor's operational limit.

The Twin Explosions: Within seconds, the intense heat fractured the fuel channels, blockading the control rods halfway down. At 01:24 AM, a colossal steam explosion ruptured the reactor vessel, blasting the 2,000-ton upper shield (the "Elena" plate) clean through the roof of the building. Seconds later, a second explosion—likely a hydrogen or chemical explosion fueled by air rushing into the superheated graphite core—shattered the remaining structure. The core was completely exposed to the open sky, igniting a massive, toxic graphite fire that burned at over 2,500°C (4,500°F).

The Immediate Aftermath: The explosions instantly killed two workers on-site and threw highly radioactive chunks of fuel and graphite chunks onto the roof of the adjacent Reactor 3. Plant operators, convinced the core was still intact due to failing, low-range dosimeters, spent hours attempting to pump water into a non-existent reactor, exposing themselves to lethal doses of radiation. Local firefighters from Pripyat arrived shortly after without any radiation gear, battling the fires on the roof to prevent the flames from spreading to the rest of the facility. Their unmatched bravery prevented a global domino effect, but doomed nearly all of the first responders to severe Acute Radiation Sickness (ARS) within hours.

November 1986

The Sarcophagus – Building the Object Shelter: Following the containment of the open-air graphite fires, the Soviet Union faced the unprecedented challenge of sealing the heavily damaged Reactor 4. The ruined structure was exposing the atmosphere to roughly 200 tons of highly radioactive corium, uranium fuel, and irradiated graphite. Named **"Object Shelter"** (Объект "Укрытие"), but universally known as the Sarcophagus, this massive concrete and steel tomb was designed under extreme time constraints. Its primary objective was simple yet terrifyingly difficult: prevent further releases of radioactive dust into the global atmosphere and shield the surrounding environment from lethal gamma radiation.

Extreme Working Conditions: The construction site was one of the most hazardous environments on Earth. Radiation levels near the ruined core were so high that standard electronics in heavy machinery instantly short-circuited, and rubber tires melted. To bypass this, engineers had to rely on modified, remote-controlled bulldozers originally designed for space exploration, alongside heavily armored cranes imported from West Germany. However, technology repeatedly failed, forcing human labor to step in. Over 600,000 workers—consisting of military conscripts, miners, and specialized engineers known as **Liquidators**—were drafted to build the shield. Many could only work in high-radiation zones for mere seconds at a time before absorbing their lifetime safe limit of radiation.

The Liquidators and "Biorobots": One of the most harrowing phases involved clearing the highly radioactive debris and graphite blocks from the roof of the adjacent Reactor 3 to prepare for the Sarcophagus assembly. When remote-controlled German vehicles failed due to the intense radiation fields, human workers, nicknamed **"biorobots,"** were deployed. Wearing makeshift, hand-cut lead aprons, these men rushed onto the roof in shifts lasting no more than 60 to 90 seconds. Armed only with shovels, they manually threw pieces of the core back into the reactor crater, facing invisible, agonizingly high doses of radiation with every step.

Innovative Engineering and Compromises: Because human crews could not safely approach the wreckage to pour standard foundations, the Sarcophagus had to be built using modular, prefabricated steel sections assembled entirely from afar using cranes equipped with TV cameras. Huge steel tubes were placed directly on top of the damaged, unstable remnants of the original Reactor 4 walls. This meant the entire 400,000 cubic meters of concrete and 7,300 tons of steel framing did not have a stable foundation; it rested on a crumbling building that had just survived a major nuclear explosion. Gaps between the modular panels were left unsealed to prevent pressure buildup, creating holes that allowed water to leak in and radioactive dust to potentially escape over time.

Completion and a Temporary Shield: On **November 30, 1986**, after just seven months of frantic, round-the-clock labor, the Soviet government officially declared the Sarcophagus complete. It succeeded in its immediate goal of stopping the catastrophic spread of airborne radiation, allowing the remaining uninjured personnel to return to work at the adjacent, undamaged Reactor 3. However, due to the rushed and remote nature of its construction, the structure was given an estimated lifespan of only 20 to 30 years. It was a ticking structural clock that would eventually require the modern world to design a permanent solution: the New Safe Confinement arch.

1991

The Reactor 2 Fire – The Beginning of the End: Five years after the catastrophe at Unit 4, the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant was still actively producing electricity despite intense local and international safety outcries. However, on **October 11, 1991**, the plant’s structural vulnerability was exposed once again. While Reactor Unit 2 was being slowed down for routine maintenance and repair work, a catastrophic mechanical failure occurred inside its Turbine Hall No. 4, triggering a massive industrial fire that threatened to compromise another nuclear core.

The Chain Reaction of Failures: The disaster began due to a faulty switch inside the turbogenerator system. A malfunction caused a sudden, uncommanded surge of electrical current to feed backwards into the generator, effectively turning it into an electric motor. The sudden mechanical stress caused the massive turbine rotor to spin wildly out of control, violently shaking itself to pieces. Shrapnel from the disintegrating turbine flew into nearby high-pressure lines, instantly rupturing them and releasing a volatile mix of highly flammable lubricating oil and hydrogen gas—the gas used to cool the generator system.

The Inferno and Roof Collapse: The escaping gases exploded into a raging inferno that quickly spread across the shared turbine hall roof. The fire raged at such intense temperatures that the heavy steel truss roof of the turbine building buckled and completely collapsed into the generator hall, burying the burning machinery under tons of debris. Because the turbine hall was structurally connected to the main reactor building, plant engineers faced the terrifying possibility that the fire could break through the thick firewall and damage the cooling systems of Reactor 2, which was still loaded with hot nuclear fuel rods.

The Emergency Response: Firefighters from Pripyat and surrounding towns were rushed back to the plant, entering highly dangerous areas with the specter of 1986 hanging over them. Fortunately, the plant’s automated emergency reactor systems functioned correctly, scramming the reactor core and pumping water to keep the fuel rods cool. The firefighters successfully contained the blaze to the turbine hall, preventing it from breaching the reactor containment building. While there was no major release of radioactive material to the environment, the structural damage to Unit 2 was devastating.

Political Fallout and Permanent Shutdown: The timing of the accident coincided with the rapid collapse of the Soviet Union. Following the disaster, the Ukrainian Parliament (**Verkhovna Rada**) faced immense pressure from Western European nations, who were terrified of another full-scale meltdown. Deeply strapped for cash and dealing with public outrage, the newly independent Ukrainian government decided that repairing the extensive damage to Reactor 2 was economically impossible. Instead of fixing the facility, they ordered the immediate, permanent decommissioning of Reactor 2, removing its fuel assemblies and reducing Chernobyl's operational capacity to just two active reactors (Units 1 and 3).

1996

The Decommissioning of Reactor 1 – Geopolitics and Global Safety: By the mid-1990s, the newly independent nation of Ukraine was caught in a severe economic crisis, heavily relying on the remaining operational units of the Chernobyl plant—Reactor 1 and Reactor 3—to supply electricity to its citizens. However, the international community, led by the G7 nations and the European Union, viewed the continued operation of any first-generation RBMK reactor as an unacceptable global risk. In December 1995, Ukraine signed a historic Memorandum of Understanding in Ottawa, agreeing to a complete shutdown of the entire facility by the year 2000 in exchange for billions of dollars in foreign loans and financial aid to upgrade its alternative energy infrastructure.

The Aging Pioneer Steps Down: As the first milestone of this international agreement, Reactor Unit 1 was earmarked for permanent closure. Having operated continuously since September 1977, Unit 1 was the oldest reactor at the complex. While it had survived the 1986 disaster unscathed and had undergone numerous post-accident safety retrofits—such as the installation of faster-acting control rods and upgraded fuel enrichment parameters—its structural components were rapidly approaching the end of their engineered lifespan. The constant bombardment of radiation had caused embrittlement of the graphite blocks and metal fuel channels inside its core, making further operations increasingly hazardous.

November 1996 – The Final Scram: In late November 1996, nuclear engineers in the Unit 1 control room began the slow, highly controlled process of reducing the thermal output of the reactor for the very last time. Unlike the chaotic events of 1986, this shutdown was a meticulous, textbook engineering procedure. Technicians systematically inserted the boron carbide control rods into the core, absorbing the neutron flux and halting the self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction. Once the core went completely cold, the turbine generators spun down to a permanent halt, officially disconnecting Unit 1 from the national power grid after 19 years of continuous service.

The Defueling Challenge: Turning off the reactor was only the first step in a decades-long decommissioning process. The immediate challenge shifted to defueling the core. Specialized crews had to safely extract all 1,600 highly radioactive spent fuel assemblies from the reactor pressure tubes using overhead fueling machines. These assemblies were then transferred to nearby cooling ponds to prevent them from overheating. This intricate operation required extreme precision, as any mechanical jam or cooling failure during the transfer could have triggered a localized radiological emergency. This successful defueling left Reactor 3 as the lone, isolated survivor producing power at the infamous site.

December 15, 2000

The Final Closure – Silencing the Complex: The definitive end of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant’s operational life arrived on December 15, 2000. Under immense pressure from the European Union, the G7, and international atomic energy watchdogs, the Ukrainian government agreed to completely halt all power generation at the site. This milestone closed a dark chapter of industrial history, turning off the last functioning unit, Reactor 3, which had continued to operate side-by-side with the radioactive tomb of Reactor 4 for over fourteen years.

The Global Broadcast and the AZ-5 Switch: The final shutdown was treated as a major geopolitical event, broadcast live via a television link to the National Palace of Arts "Ukraina" in Kyiv. There, Ukrainian President Leonid Kuchma stood before a large audience of international diplomats and dignitaries. At exactly 1:15 PM local time, President Kuchma issued the formal direct order via radio to the engineers waiting inside the Reactor 3 control room. The shift supervisor turned the master key and pressed the emergency shutdown button (AZ-5), triggering the rapid insertion of the control rods into the core to permanently stop the nuclear chain reaction. Within seconds, the turbine generators spun down, and the site officially stopped producing electricity.

The Irony of Reactor 3: Reactor 3 was structurally identical to the ill-fated Reactor 4, separated only by a thick concrete firewall inside the same mega-structure. Following the 1986 disaster, Unit 3 had been contaminated by radioactive dust drawn into its ventilation systems. It required months of intensive scrubbing, chemical decontamination, and the complete replacement of its air filtering systems before Soviet authorities could safely order workers back inside. For over a decade, hundreds of plant employees walked through the shadow of the Sarcophagus every single day just to reach their stations at Unit 3, a surreal daily routine that highlighted Ukraine's desperate need for energy.

Transition to a Decommissioning Site: Shutting down the reactors did not mean the plant was abandoned. The event marked the transformation of Chernobyl from an active power station into one of the largest, most complex nuclear decommissioning projects on Earth. Thousands of specialized workers were required to remain on-site to oversee the decades-long process of safely removing the highly radioactive spent fuel rods, processing liquid radioactive waste, and maintaining the structural integrity of the decaying 1986 Sarcophagus.

The Legacy of the Plant: Over its active lifespan from 1977 to 2000, the Chernobyl complex generated over 300 billion kilowatt-hours of electricity. However, its true legacy became the profound lessons it forced upon the global nuclear industry, reshaping international safety protocols, reactor design principles, and emergency response frameworks forever. The final turn of the key in 2000 left the entire Chernobyl Exclusion Zone silent, cementing its transition from a triumph of Soviet engineering into a permanent, heavily guarded monument to the atomic age.

2000 - 2013

The Preparatory Phase and Defueling: Following the complete shutdown of the plant, the newly formed State Specialized Enterprise (SSE ChNPP) launched a comprehensive, multi-stage decommissioning strategy approved by the Ukrainian government [Ukraine Draft Law, Yenisafak]. The primary focus of this decade was the highly delicate process of completely defueling the remaining operational lines (Reactors 1, 2, and 3) [Yenisafak, Wikipedia]. This required extracting thousands of highly radioactive fuel assemblies and safely moving them into massive, newly engineered, long-term specialized spent-fuel storage facilities (ISF) [Yenisafak, Wikipedia]. Concurrently, global focus turned toward a critical crisis: the original 1986 Sarcophagus was rapidly decaying, with gaps allowing rainwater to leak in, accelerating structural corrosion and raising fears of a catastrophic roof collapse [Groki, ChNPP].

2010 - 2016

The New Safe Confinement (NSC) Construction: To avert a secondary global radiological disaster, an international coalition financed the creation of the New Safe Confinement (NSC)—the largest movable land structure ever built by mankind [Wikipedia, YouTube]. Spanning 257 meters wide, 108 meters tall, and weighing over 36,000 tonnes, this mega-arch was designed to last at least 100 years [World Nucl, Wikipedia]. Because the radiation fields directly above Reactor 4 were still completely lethal, the colossal steel arch had to be constructed in two halves in a cleared, safer zone exactly 330 meters away [World Nucl, ChX]. In November 2016, using an intricate network of hydraulic jacks and Teflon rails, the monumental structure was slowly slid over the old Sarcophagus, completely sealing the radioactive ruins from the outside world [Wikipedia, ChX].

2019

Commissioning the Arc and Initial Disassembly: After years of complex system integration—including advanced ventilation networks to manage humidity and prevent the internal rusting of the 1986 frame—the New Safe Confinement was officially handed over to Ukrainian authorities and fully commissioned [World Nucl, Wikipedia]. The internal space of the arch is equipped with massive, automated overhead cranes [ChX]. These heavy machines were explicitly installed to perform the next monumental task: carefully dismantling the unstable, upper sections of the old 1986 Sarcophagus from the inside out, preventing an uncontrolled collapse while capturing any radioactive dust particles before they can escape [YouTube, ChX].

2022

Geopolitical Crisis and Occupation: In February 2022, the Chernobyl Exclusion Zone became a frontline military zone following a full-scale invasion [World Nucl]. Russian forces captured the nuclear power plant, holding the shift workers and national guardsmen hostage at gunpoint for 36 days. The heavy military vehicles stirred up radioactive dust from the highly contaminated soil, causing a temporary spike in local radiation readings. The plant experienced a critical power outage, forcing emergency diesel generators to temporarily run the cooling systems for the spent nuclear fuel storage pools. Ukrainian engineers maintained the facility under extreme duress until military forces withdrew in late March 2022, leaving behind looted laboratories and damaged radiation monitoring equipment.

Present Day (2026)

The Ongoing Decommissioning Extension: Today, the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant operates under an extended, updated decommissioning blueprint slated to run until 2036 and beyond [World Nucl]. The plant maintains a large daily workforce of scientists, technicians, and security personnel tasked with managing the highly complex, ongoing extraction of nuclear materials [Wikipedia]. The final objective remains the "Brown spot" state targeted for completion around 2065, a long-term goal where all contaminated equipment, including the melted lava-like fuel inside Reactor 4, will be fully dismantled, packed into deep geological repositories, and the site is finally released from heavy regulatory control [ChNPP].
