Historical biography

Volodymyr Pravyk

As the Lieutenant and Watch Commander of the Para-military Fire Brigade 2 (SVPCh-2) at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant on the night of April 26, 1986, his immediate, fearless leadership in commanding the first responders on the scene, his critical decision to alert the entire region for reinforcements, and his desperate struggle to contain the fires on the highly radioactive turbine hall roof, made him one of the most heroic and tragic figures of the nuclear disaster.

Birth: 13th of June 1962
Death: 11th of May 1986

First Years and Formation

Born on June 13, 1962, in Chernobyl, Kyiv Oblast (within the Ukrainian SSR), Volodymyr Pavlovych Pravyk chose a selfless life path dedicated to public safety. He graduated from secondary school in 1979 and immediately enrolled in the Cherkasy Fire-Technical School of the Ministry of Internal Affairs, graduating with the rank of Lieutenant of the Internal Service. Upon graduation, he was assigned as a Watch Commander for the Paramilitary Fire Brigade No. 2 (SVPCh-2), the specialized unit tasked with the high-stakes responsibility of protecting the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant and the growing city of Pripyat. He settled nearby, having recently married his wife, Natalya, with whom he had a newborn daughter.

Chernobyl Carreer

Pravyk's career at the Chernobyl station was defined by professional excellence, rapid decision-making, and deep camaraderie with his crew. Despite his youth—being only 23 years old in the spring of 1986—he earned the respect of both seasoned plant engineers and his fellow firefighters through his rigorous approach to tactical industrial firefighting. He possessed an intricate knowledge of the plant's massive layout, structural materials, and high-voltage hazards. On the night of April 25-26, it was supposed to be his day off, but he chose to swap shifts with his friend and colleague, Pyotr Khmel, placing his watch directly in the path of history.

Day of the disaster

When Reactor 4 exploded at 1:23 a.m. on April 26, 1986, Pravyk and his third watch felt the shockwave from their station less than a kilometer away. Without waiting for an official alarm, they geared up and drove their fire engines straight toward the towering column of smoke and glowing debris. Upon arrival, Pravyk immediately recognized that his small crew was facing an unmitigated catastrophe. Showing remarkable composure, he bypassed standard procedures to issue a "No. 3 Alert"—a critical radioactive emergency signal that summoned every fire brigade across the Kyiv Oblast, Pripyat, and Chernobyl regions to the plant.

Mitigation actions and Death

Unaware that the reactor core lay completely exposed, Pravyk led his men into lethal radiation fields to battle intense fires fueled by combustible bitumen on the roof of the turbine hall and around the ventilation stack. Wearing only standard cotton uniforms and canvas gear, they worked alongside Lieutenant Viktor Kibenok's crew, preventing the flames from spreading to the intact Reactor 3. During these desperate hours, Pravyk inhaled toxic smoke laced with radioactive isotopes and suffered severe external beta burns. Evacuated to Moscow Hospital Number 6 on April 27, he suffered from acute radiation syndrome (ARS). Despite a agonizing battle against internal organ failure and skin tissue degradation, Volodymyr Pravyk died on May 11, 1986. He was posthumously awarded the title Hero of the Soviet Union.
