Who was he and what role he had?

Aleksandr Akimov was a Soviet nuclear engineer who served as the Unit Shift Supervisor (NSB—Начальник Смены Блока). His main responsibility was to supervise all operations of Unit 4 during his shift. He directly managed all the control room operators, including Leonid Toptunov.

Early life and formation

He was born in Novosibirsk, Russia. Like many of the Soviet Union's most talented engineers, he moved to the capital to pursue higher education. In 1976, he graduated with honors from the Moscow Power Engineering Institute (MPEI), obtaining a specialist degree in the Automation of Heat and Power Engineering Processes.

Chernobyl Carreer

Akimov started working at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in September 1979. During his first years, he held the positions of Senior Turbine Management Engineer and Shift Supervisor of the Turbine Hall. On July 10, 1984, he was promoted to Unit Shift Supervisor (NSB) of Reactor Block 4, a role of high responsibility that demonstrated his solid technical career within the power plant.

Day of the disaster

On the night of April 25–26, Akimov assumed leadership of the night shift. The reactor had experienced severe delays in its scheduled safety test, causing a phenomenon known as xenon poisoning. Akimov initially opposed carrying out the procedure due to the low power and inherent instability—it is important to note that RBMK reactors are extremely unstable at low power levels—which left the reactor in a precarious state just as the SIUR, Leonid Toptunov, took over the controls.

Nonetheless, after receiving intense pressure and threats of professional repercussions from his superior, the Deputy Chief Engineer for Operations, Anatoly Dyatlov, Akimov had no choice but to proceed and ordered the test to carry on. At 1:23:40 AM, seeing that the reactor parameters were scaling dangerously, Akimov ordered the emergency shutdown button (SCRAM), known as AZ-5, to be pressed in order to fully insert the control rods and shut down the reactor. Under normal circumstances, this shutdown would have been successful; however, a severe design flaw in the reactor triggered a catastrophic power surge, leading to the explosions just five seconds after the button was pressed.

Mitigation Actions and Death

Immediately after the explosion, Akimov went into a state of shock and disbelief; during the first few hours, he mistakenly reported that the reactor was still intact, a conclusion based on the failure of the control room's measuring devices. Nevertheless, once he assimilated the true magnitude of the disaster, he prioritized saving his team and containing the damage.

Along with Leonid Toptunov, he went to room 714/2, where the valves designed to open the water flow to the reactor were located. During those hours, he was exposed to a lethal dose of gamma radiation. They received help from Vyacheslav Orlov, Arkady Uskov, and Aleksandr Nekhayev, but these men spent less time there, absorbing less radiation, and survived.

Because of those efforts, he absorbed a lethal dose of radiation (between 13 Gy and 15 Gy). He was evacuated to Pripyat's hospital (located on Lenin Avenue) and was later sent to Hospital Number 6 in Moscow, where his body deteriorated quickly due to Acute Radiation Syndrome. He spent his final days defending his team's actions, repeatedly telling his family members: "We did everything right; I cannot understand why that happened." He died on May 11, 1986, with radiation burns covering 100% of his body.

"Until his last breath, Akimov insisted that his control room team had followed the emergency protocols established by the plant's manuals."
