Historical biography

Valery Perevozchenko

As the Shift Foreman of the Reactor Section (nachalnik smeny reaktornogo tsekha) at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant on the night of April 26, 1986, his desperate search through highly radioactive ruins to rescue his trapped operators, and his early, accurate assessment of the destroyed reactor core, made him one of the most critical and tragic figures of the nuclear disaster. Valery Perevozchenko was directly responsible for the safe and proper operation of the reactor shop's equipment. On his shift, he supervised the technicians and engineers monitoring the physical state of Reactor 4. When the explosions tore the block apart, his primary duty shifted from operational management to an immediate, frantic rescue mission to save his crew members scattered throughout the collapsing, highly toxic structure.

Birth: 6th of May 1947
Death: 13th of June 1986

First Years and Formation

Born on May 6, 1947, in Starodub, Bryansk Oblast (within the Russian SFSR), Valery Perevozchenko chose a career path dedicated to the military and nuclear technology. He graduated from the prestigious Higher Naval Engineering School in Sevastopol, specializing as a naval engineer with a focus on shipboard nuclear power plants. Before arriving at Chernobyl, he served as a nuclear submarine officer in the Soviet Northern Fleet, a background that gave him deep technical expertise in reactor physics and radiation safety under extreme pressure. He eventually settled in Pripyat with his wife and two daughters to join the civilian nuclear program.

Chernobyl Carreer

Perevozchenko joined the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in 1981, bringing years of military discipline and rigorous nuclear reactor experience with him. He started working in the reactor shop and was quickly promoted to Shift Foreman due to his competence and calm authority. By April 1986, he was a highly respected supervisor, trusted by both the senior management and the young operators under his command. He knew every corridor, pipe, and valve of Reactor 4, a familiarity that proved crucial when the plant became a dark, twisted labyrinth on the night of the accident.

Day of the disaster

On the night of April 26, 1986, Perevozchenko was stationed in the reactor section. Shortly after the fatal button AZ-5 was pressed, the initial steam explosions tore through Reactor 4. As power was lost and dust choked the corridors, Perevozchenko immediately rushed into Control Room 4 to report a catastrophic site: the roof of the reactor hall had completely collapsed. Realizing the immediate danger his operators faced, he refused to evacuate and instead chose to head back into the heavily damaged, pitch-black structure to ensure his team's safety.

Mitigation actions and Death

Driven by a deep sense of duty, Perevozchenko entered lethal radiation fields to search for missing personnel, most notably his friend and senior pump operator, Valery Khodemchuk, who was trapped near the shattered main circulation pumps. Along with engineers Aleksandr Kudryavtsev and Viktor Proskuryakov, Perevozchenko also attempted to manually lower the reactor's control rods in a desperate, futile bid to halt the nuclear reaction. During these actions, he was exposed directly to the open reactor core, sustaining massive radiation burns. Evacuated to Moscow Hospital Number 6, Valery Perevozchenko succumbed to Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS) on June 13, 1986, sacrificing his life to save his crew.
